GDPR's Impact on Privacy Policies

Welcome to our in-depth exploration of GDPR's impact on privacy policies. With PrivacyLens, we track the evolution of these policies before and after GDPR's implementation by tracking Policy changes over time.

Policy Updates

In order to keep users informed of changes in data collection and usage, policy updates are crucial. Our analysis categorized policies into two groups - device-specific and non-device-specific. Out of the 462 policies analyzed, only 284 indicated when they were last updated. Charts shown below illustrates the updates made on each device type privacy policy over the years. Red line in both the charts depicts as a boundary to check updates on policies before and after GDPR | CCPA happened.

Note: Red boundary line in the below charts represents the year when GDPR happened

We observed that around 88% of policies were revised following the introduction of GDPR and CCPA in 2018. This could be due to manufacturers recognizing the importance of incorporating new guidelines and responsibilities brought about by GDPR and CCPA, resulting in a surge of updates to privacy policies. The results emphasize the significance of policy updates and the necessity of keeping policies current with the most recent laws and regulations to guarantee the safeguarding and proper management of user data.

Here we illustrate the importance of data privacy regulations worldwide, which require websites and devices to comply with strict laws on collecting and storing user data. Failure to comply can result in heavy fines and prosecution. The study investigated privacy policies from different countries and found that the USA was the most prominent manufacturer of IoT devices, with smart home devices being the most common type, followed by China with smart home devices and smart scales. Other countries, including Germany, Japan, France, and the UK, also had a significant contribution to the devices studied.

The chart below represents the distribution of privacy policies into two categories based on whether the name of a smart device is mentioned or not, for each of the smart device types that we analyzed.

We found that some device categories, such as smart cameras, lights, watches, sensors, and home devices, were more prevalent than others like smart gaming equipment, projectors, body scanners, and mounts. Fewer options were available in the latter categories, and our analysis showed that their privacy policies were significantly worse than those with more manufacturers. This could be due to monopolistic conditions leading to less effort in policy development. A clear correlation was observed between the number of manufacturers and privacy policy quality. Our analysis also identified significant gaps in the information provided by smart device privacy policies, particularly among European manufacturers, where 60% didn't mention how they handle data collected from devices. This highlights the need for better policies that inform users about data collection practices.
