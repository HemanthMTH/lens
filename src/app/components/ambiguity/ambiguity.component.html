Ambiguity

In our research, eight metrics represented the policies regarding key information. To classify policies based on these metrics, we used a supervised learning algorithm known as the random forest classifier. Supervised learning is a reliable approach for classification tasks but requires a significant portion of the dataset to be labeled. Therefore, we partially annotated 100 policies in our dataset, which was sufficient for the random forest classifier to work well for our dataset.

Our research concentrated on how ambiguity affects the effectiveness of classification methods.We found that these algorithms were less accurate for highly nebulous programs, as they have less well-defined conditions and guidelines. Our results highlighted the importance of policy nebulosity as a factor affecting classification models' performance, suggesting further research is needed to improve algorithms that can handle nebulous programs.

The classification of policy texts becomes more difficult for ambiguous policies, as evidenced by a decrease in F1 scores from 86% to 67% with increasing ambiguity (shown below). Logistic regression also saw a drop in F1 scores from 77% to 69% for ambiguous policies. Classification algorithms struggle with accuracy when dealing with highly ambiguous policies. The accuracy of these models varies across ambiguity categories, and further research is required to develop algorithms that can effectively classify ambiguous policies.

We found that almost all smart-connected car policies were classified as very ambiguous, indicating a need for clearer information. Our analysis showed EU privacy policies to be less ambiguous than those in the US and China, likely due to stricter data privacy laws like GDPR. We categorized policies into three ambiguity levels: not-ambiguous, somewhat-ambiguous, and very-ambiguous. The EU had the highest percentage of not-ambiguous policies (65.8%), while China had the lowest (54.3%). The US had the most somewhat-ambiguous policies (27.5%), with the EU having the least (12.5%). In the very-ambiguous category, China had the highest percentage (23.9%), and the US had the lowest (18.7%) as shown below.

Overall, the results suggest that companies need to pay more attention to the level of ambiguity in their privacy policies, particularly for smart-connected cars. The findings also highlight the need for stricter regulations and guidelines to ensure that privacy policies are more transparent and easier to understand for users.
